COSMIC TIMELINES

Journey Through Time and Space

Formation of the Universe

From the Big Bang to the present day, explore the 13.8 billion year journey of our cosmos.

0 seconds

The Big Bang

The universe begins as an infinitely hot, dense point. Space and time itself are created. All matter and energy emerge from this singular moment.

10⁻⁴³ seconds

Planck Epoch

The earliest moment we can describe with physics. The four fundamental forces (gravity, electromagnetic, strong, and weak) are unified.

10⁻³⁶ seconds

Cosmic Inflation

The universe expands exponentially, growing from smaller than an atom to the size of a grapefruit in a fraction of a second.

3 minutes

Nucleosynthesis

The universe cools enough for protons and neutrons to combine, forming the first atomic nuclei: hydrogen and helium.

380,000 years

Recombination & First Light

Electrons combine with nuclei to form neutral atoms. The universe becomes transparent, releasing the cosmic microwave background radiation we detect today.

200 million years

First Stars Form

Gravity pulls together clouds of hydrogen and helium, igniting the first generation of stars. These massive stars light up the dark universe.

1 billion years

First Galaxies

Stars cluster together under gravity, forming the first galaxies. These early galaxies are smaller and more chaotic than modern ones.

9 billion years

Solar System Forms

Our Sun ignites from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust. Planets, including Earth, coalesce from the remaining material.

13.8 billion years

Present Day

The universe continues to expand, with billions of galaxies scattered across the observable cosmos. We exist in this moment of cosmic history.

Star Life Cycle

From birth in stellar nurseries to spectacular deaths, follow the journey of stars through the cosmos.

Nebula

Beginning

A giant cloud of gas and dust in space. Gravity begins to pull the material together, starting the star formation process.

→

Protostar

~100,000 years

The collapsing cloud heats up, forming a dense core. Not yet hot enough for nuclear fusion, but glowing from gravitational energy.

→

Main Sequence Star

Millions to Billions of years

Nuclear fusion begins! Hydrogen fuses into helium in the core. The star is stable, balancing gravity with fusion pressure. Our Sun is here.

Key Facts

  • 90% of a star's life is spent in the main sequence phase
  • All elements heavier than hydrogen and helium were forged in stars
  • The atoms in your body were once inside a star
  • Our Sun is about halfway through its 10 billion year main sequence life
  • Red giants can be 100 times larger than their main sequence size
